🌦 About weather models

Models differ in two ways that matter for flying: grid resolution (how finely they see Utah's ridges and canyons) and forecast skill (how well they nail the incoming weather). No single model wins both, so the order below is the accuracy ranking for the mountain west, best first.

1HRRR~1.9 mi grid · updates hourly

Sharpest terrain detail and refreshed every hour, so it best captures Utah's valley/ridge winds and thermal timing. Use it for today & tomorrow. Short-range only — it runs out at ~48 hours, after which the app extends the strip with GFS.

2ECMWF~16 mi grid

The most skillful global model in the world. Coarser grid smooths the mountains, but it's the best at front timing and the multi-day trend. The go-to for days 2–7. Pair it with HRRR: HRRR near-term, ECMWF for the outlook.

3ICON~7 mi grid

Germany's DWD model — rated just behind ECMWF for global accuracy, and its finer grid resolves terrain better than ECMWF/GFS. A strong middle ground: more terrain awareness than ECMWF, longer range than HRRR.

4GEM~9 mi grid

Canada's global model. Respectably high-resolution and reliable, but less often the standout in the western US. Useful as a tiebreaker vote when the others disagree.

5GFS~16 mi grid

The American global model — free, dependable, and what extends this app's 2-week forecast. Verifies a notch below ECMWF, especially for the timing and strength of systems. Fine for the broad picture.

Best matchblend

Open-Meteo automatically stitches the best-available model for each location and range. Convenient, but it hides which model is talking — so you lose the ability to judge agreement.

💡 The pro habit: open ⚖ Compare and watch for agreement. When HRRR, ECMWF and ICON line up, confidence is high; when they diverge, treat the day as uncertain. And remember — every model smooths the terrain, so none truly capture a specific launch's rotor, venturi, or thermal trigger. That gap is what local knowledge and an on-site wind check fill.
🪂 About launch details

Each launch card packs a lot into one screen. Here's what every row means and how to read it.

Facing & wind window

The launch's aspect (e.g. W-facing 270°) and the arc of directions considered flyable. Surface wind arriving from outside that arc is cross or tail — not safe to inflate on. The colored wedge on direction cells shows the arc.

Ideal / Max / Gust

idealLo–idealHi is the sweet spot for surface wind. max is the hard ceiling. gust is the maximum acceptable gust spread (gust minus wind). Together these drive the Fly / Caution / No-Fly score.

P-rating (P2 / P3 / P4)

USHPA rating required. P2 for novices, P3 intermediate, P4 advanced. If a site is marked P4, don't fly it below rating even if the numbers look green.

Launch alt / LZ alt / Vertical

Launch alt = MSL elevation at the takeoff. LZ alt = MSL of the primary landing zone. Vertical = the drop between them, which is your altitude budget for the flight down.

Glide required

Minimum glide ratio needed to reach the LZ from launch. If forecast headwind pushes your working glide below this number, the site is a no-go for that day regardless of thermic conditions.

Density altitude

The effective altitude your wing feels after correcting for temperature and pressure. Higher DA = less lift, longer takeoff/landing rolls, softer flare. More than ~3,000 ft above field elevation is a red flag — plan for a fast forward launch and a firm landing.

Est. cloudbase

Working ceiling for thermals — the altitude MSL at which rising air cools to the dewpoint and forms cumulus. "Above launch" is your headroom; if cloudbase is at or below launch altitude, you're launching into cloud and you shouldn't.

Thermal top

Peak MSL altitude the mixed convective layer is forecast to reach today — the model's boundary-layer depth added to launch elevation. On blue days this IS your working ceiling. On cu days, real ceiling = min(thermal top, cloudbase). "Peak 2pm" tells you when the day tops out.

Thermal window

The hours the boundary layer is deep enough (≥1,640 ft AGL) to sustain useable thermals — think of it as "thermals on" → "thermals off." The trigger temperature next to it is the surface temp today needs to hit for the morning inversion to break. Plan XC launches inside this window.

Day type

Quick classifier from cloudbase vs thermal top. Cu day (green): cumulus forms right around the BL top — thermals are visually marked from a distance. Blue day (blue): cloudbase is above the BL top or air's too dry — thermals still work but nothing marks them. Capped (amber): cu are forming well below BL top — usually means the day will overdevelop into storms.

Wind aloft (hodograph)

Wind speed & direction at a stack of altitudes above the site right now. Each colored dot is one level, labeled with its altitude. A straight line = steady flow all the way up. A curving line = the wind turns as you climb (drift & shear — expect more bumps and cross-country push).

Wind rose

How today's daytime surface wind is distributed by direction. Longer spoke = more hours from that way; color = average speed. The blue ● marks the launch facing — you want the busiest spokes near that dot.

Hazards / Airspace / Radio / Access

Free-text notes: transmission lines, rotor zones, airspace ceilings, active gun ranges, 4WD access, unicom radio channels, best-window advice. Read these before every unfamiliar flight.

💡 Reading the whole card: Fly / No-Fly at the top is the quick verdict. The Ideal/Max/Gust row explains the surface score. The Wind-aloft panel tells you what the climb will be like. Density altitude + cloudbase set your realistic ceiling. Always sanity-check against the hazard list before committing.
⚙ Fly-score thresholds
Each parameter has a caution edge and a no-fly edge. For higher-bad params (like wind), values at or above caution demote to caution and at or above no-fly veto. Lower-bad params (like cloud base, visibility) work the other way. The composite fly-score is a weighted average — weights only count for params the app has live data for; the rest are stored for when a data source is added.
