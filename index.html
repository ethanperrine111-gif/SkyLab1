🌦 About weather models

Models differ in two ways that matter for flying: grid resolution (how finely they see Utah's ridges and canyons) and forecast skill (how well they nail the incoming weather). No single model wins both, so the order below is the accuracy ranking for the mountain west, best first.

1HRRR~1.9 mi grid · updates hourly

Sharpest terrain detail and refreshed every hour, so it best captures Utah's valley/ridge winds and thermal timing. Use it for today & tomorrow. Short-range only — it runs out at ~48 hours, after which the app extends the strip with GFS.

2ECMWF~16 mi grid

The most skillful global model in the world. Coarser grid smooths the mountains, but it's the best at front timing and the multi-day trend. The go-to for days 2–7. Pair it with HRRR: HRRR near-term, ECMWF for the outlook.

3ICON~7 mi grid

Germany's DWD model — rated just behind ECMWF for global accuracy, and its finer grid resolves terrain better than ECMWF/GFS. A strong middle ground: more terrain awareness than ECMWF, longer range than HRRR.

4GEM~9 mi grid

Canada's global model. Respectably high-resolution and reliable, but less often the standout in the western US. Useful as a tiebreaker vote when the others disagree.

5GFS~16 mi grid

The American global model — free, dependable, and what extends this app's 2-week forecast. Verifies a notch below ECMWF, especially for the timing and strength of systems. Fine for the broad picture.

Best matchblend

Open-Meteo automatically stitches the best-available model for each location and range. Convenient, but it hides which model is talking — so you lose the ability to judge agreement.

💡 The pro habit: open ⚖ Compare and watch for agreement. When HRRR, ECMWF and ICON line up, confidence is high; when they diverge, treat the day as uncertain. And remember — every model smooths the terrain, so none truly capture a specific launch's rotor, venturi, or thermal trigger. That gap is what local knowledge and an on-site wind check fill.
